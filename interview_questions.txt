 SECTION 1: BASIC LEVEL QUESTIONS
 (Understanding concepts, definitions, and purpose)
================================================================================

--- TOPIC: Project Overview ---

Q1.  What is the main goal of your thesis project?
A:   To detect deforestation in the Amazon rainforest automatically by applying
     a deep learning model (UTAE) on Sentinel-1 SAR satellite image time series,
     outperforming traditional statistical and machine learning methods.

Q2.  What does "deforestation detection" mean in the context of this project?
A:   It means identifying pixels in satellite images that transitioned from
     "forested" to "deforested" state over a given time period — essentially
     a change detection problem.

Q3.  Why is Amazon deforestation monitoring important?
A:   Because the Amazon is the world's largest rainforest. Its loss accelerates
     global warming, causes irreversible biodiversity damage, disrupts the water
     cycle, and affects indigenous communities.

Q4.  What does SAR stand for, and why is it used here?
A:   Synthetic Aperture Radar. It uses microwave signals to image the Earth's
     surface, works both day and night, and — crucially — penetrates cloud cover.
     This makes it ideal for tropical regions like the Amazon, which are
     frequently covered by clouds.

Q5.  What satellite provides the SAR data used in this project?
A:   Sentinel-1, part of the ESA (European Space Agency) Copernicus programme.
     It operates in the C-Band microwave frequency.

Q6.  What are the two SAR polarization channels used, and what are their
     typical value ranges in this project?
A:   - VV (Vertical transmit / Vertical receive): approx. -20 dB to +5 dB
     - VH (Vertical transmit / Horizontal receive): approx. -26 dB to -1 dB

Q7.  What is the name of the dataset created for this project?
A:   BraDD-S1TS (Brazilian Deforestation Detection - Sentinel-1 Time Series).
     It contains approximately 25,000 image sequences from the Brazilian Amazon.

Q8.  Where is the BraDD-S1TS dataset publicly available?
A:   On Zenodo (https://zenodo.org/record/8060250).

Q9.  What is a time series in the context of satellite imagery?
A:   A sequence of satellite images taken over the same geographic area at
     different points in time, allowing the model to observe how the landscape
     changes over months or years.

Q10. What is the primary evaluation metric used to measure model performance?
A:   IoU — Intersection over Union (also known as the Jaccard Index).
     It measures the overlap between predicted deforestation masks and ground truth.

Q11. By how many percentage points does the proposed model outperform
     existing methods in IoU?
A:   13.7 percentage points in IoU.

Q12. What are the two output classes the model predicts?
A:   Forest (unchanged) and Deforested (changed/cleared).

Q13. What framework is used to train the model?
A:   PyTorch and PyTorch Lightning.

Q14. What tool is used for experiment tracking and logging?
A:   Weights & Biases (WandB) and TensorBoard.

Q15. What is the entry point of the project code?
A:   run_via_parser.py — it handles command-line argument parsing, logger
     setup, trainer initialization, checkpointing, training, and testing.


--- TOPIC: Deep Learning Basics ---

Q16. What is a neural network?
A:   A computational model inspired by the human brain, composed of layers of
     interconnected nodes (neurons) that learn to map inputs to outputs by
     adjusting their weights through training.

Q17. What is the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning?
A:   In supervised learning, the model is trained on labeled input-output pairs.
     In unsupervised learning, no labels are provided; the model finds patterns
     on its own. This project uses supervised learning (labeled deforestation masks).

Q18. What is a loss function?
A:   A function that measures the error between the model's predictions and the
     ground truth labels. The optimizer minimizes this loss during training.

Q19. What loss functions are used in this project?
A:   CrossEntropy Loss, Dice Loss, and Focal Loss (defined in source/loss/).

Q20. What optimizer is used and what does it do?
A:   AdamW — a variant of Adam optimizer with weight decay for regularization.
     It adaptively adjusts learning rates per parameter.

Q21. What is a batch in deep learning training?
A:   A subset of the training data processed together in a single forward and
     backward pass. Using mini-batches (instead of the full dataset) makes
     training computationally feasible.

Q22. What is data normalization and why is it important?
A:   Scaling input data to a standard range/distribution (e.g., zero mean, unit
     variance). It speeds up training convergence and prevents certain features
     from dominating due to scale differences.

Q23. What normalization methods are implemented in this project?
A:   ZScore (mean/std normalization), MinMax normalization, and a domain-specific
     SAR normalization based on fixed VV/VH value ranges.

Q24. What is overfitting?
A:   When a model learns the training data too well — including its noise — and
     fails to generalize to unseen data. Signs: high training accuracy,
     low validation/test accuracy.

Q25. What is data augmentation?
A:   Techniques applied to training data to artificially increase its diversity
     (e.g., flipping, rotation, temporal dropout), which helps the model generalize.

 SECTION 2: MEDIUM LEVEL QUESTIONS
 (Architecture, pipeline, and implementation details)
================================================================================

--- TOPIC: Model Architecture (UTAE) ---

Q26. What does UTAE stand for?
A:   U-Net with Temporal Attention Encoder. It is an attention-based architecture
     designed to process spatial-temporal satellite image sequences.

Q27. What is U-Net and where does it come from originally?
A:   U-Net is a convolutional neural network architecture originally designed for
     biomedical image segmentation. It features a symmetric encoder-decoder
     structure with skip connections that preserve spatial detail.

Q28. What is the role of the encoder in U-Net?
A:   The encoder (contracting path) progressively downsamples the input image
     to extract high-level feature representations at multiple scales.

Q29. What is the role of the decoder in U-Net?
A:   The decoder (expanding path) upsamples the feature maps back to the original
     resolution, combining them with skip connections from the encoder to
     produce a precise segmentation mask.

Q30. What makes UTAE different from standard U-Net?
A:   UTAE includes a Temporal Attention Encoder that processes sequences of images
     over time. It uses attention weights to focus on the most relevant time steps
     for a given prediction, making it suitable for time-series satellite imagery.

Q31. What is an attention mechanism in neural networks?
A:   A mechanism that allows the model to assign different importance weights to
     different parts of the input (spatial regions or temporal steps), helping
     it focus on what is most relevant for the prediction.

Q32. Why is temporal attention especially important for deforestation detection?
A:   Because deforestation is a temporal event — it is identified by change over
     time. Temporal attention lets the model focus on the time steps where
     significant land cover changes occurred, rather than treating all images equally.

Q33. What are the input dimensions to the Network's forward pass?
A:   - images:      [B, T, C, H, W] — Batch, Time steps, Channels, Height, Width
     - days:        [B, T]          — acquisition day (relative) for each image
     - target_days: [B, t]          — target label days


--- TOPIC: Forward Function Strategies ---

Q34. What are the three forward function types implemented?
A:   1. segment_wise   — Processes sub-sequences for each consecutive pair of
                         target days, using a +/-30-day error buffer.
     2. begin_to_end   — Processes images from the first to the last target day
                         as a single sequence.
     3. use_all_info   — Uses all available images regardless of target days
                         (vanilla/baseline approach).

Q35. What is the purpose of the _error_days = 30 parameter?
A:   It adds a +/-30-day buffer around the target label dates when selecting which
     images to include in a sub-sequence. This accounts for timing uncertainty
     in when deforestation actually occurred vs. when it was labeled.

Q36. Explain how _segment_wise works step by step.
A:   For each consecutive pair of target days (start, end):
     1. Expand the window: start -= 30, end += 30
     2. Create a boolean mask of images falling within that window
     3. Extract those images and their day timestamps
     4. Pass them through the underlying UTAE network -> get a [B, L, H, W] mask
     5. Stack all segment outputs -> final shape [B, t-1, L, H, W]

Q37. What is change detection and how is it implemented here?
A:   Change detection determines whether a pixel's state changed between two
     time steps (e.g., Forest -> Deforested). In forward_function.py, the
     _change_detection logic compares predictions across consecutive timestamps
     to produce a binary change map (change target: 0 = no change, 1 = changed).


--- TOPIC: Dataset & Data Pipeline ---

Q38. What are the two data splits in BraDD-S1TS and what do they represent?
A:   "close" and "far" splits — they refer to spatial proximity of test
     samples to training samples, evaluating geographic generalization ability.

Q39. What does TemporalDropout do and why is it useful?
A:   It randomly or deterministically reduces the number of time steps in a
     sequence during training. Random temporal dropout acts as data augmentation,
     improving model generalization by training with variable-length sequences.

Q40. How are missing/padding timestamps handled in the dataset?
A:   Timestamps (ImageDays) start at 1 and use 0-padding for missing data,
     allowing variable-length sequences to be batched together.

Q41. What is collate_tensor used for in the data module?
A:   To combine tensors of variable sequence lengths into a single batch tensor
     (applying padding where necessary), since different samples may have
     different numbers of images in their time series.

Q42. Why are statistics calculated only on the training set?
A:   To prevent data leakage — if normalization statistics are computed including
     the validation/test set, the model indirectly "sees" test data, leading to
     overoptimistic performance estimates.

Q43. What does meta.csv contain, and how is it used?
A:   It contains metadata for each sample in the dataset, including a "file"
     column (the path to the .pt file) and split columns (e.g., close_set,
     far_set) indicating whether a sample belongs to train/val/test.


--- TOPIC: Training & Evaluation ---

Q44. What is PyTorch Lightning and why was it used?
A:   PyTorch Lightning is a high-level wrapper around PyTorch that abstracts
     away boilerplate code (training loops, logging, checkpointing, GPU
     management), allowing researchers to focus on model development.

Q45. What happens during a training_step in the Experiment class?
A:   A batch of SAR images is passed through the Network. The ForwardFunction
     computes predictions, which are compared to targets via the loss function.
     The loss is backpropagated and the optimizer updates the model weights.

Q46. What does IoU (Intersection over Union) measure, and how is it calculated?
A:   IoU measures the overlap between the predicted mask and the ground truth:
     IoU = (Predicted INTERSECT Ground Truth) / (Predicted UNION Ground Truth)
     A value of 1.0 = perfect prediction; 0.0 = no overlap.

Q47. Why is IoU preferred over simple pixel accuracy for this task?
A:   Because deforestation is rare — most pixels remain forest. Simple accuracy
     is misleading (a model predicting "all forest" would score very high).
     IoU penalizes models that miss the deforested class.

Q48. What is a learning rate scheduler and is one used here?
A:   A scheduler adjusts the learning rate during training (e.g., decay after
     each epoch). The Experiment class includes an LR scheduler for AdamW.

Q49. What is model checkpointing and why is it important?
A:   Saving the model's weights at certain points during training (e.g., when
     validation IoU improves). It ensures you can restore the best-performing
     model even if training continues to overfit.

 SECTION 3: DIFFICULT LEVEL QUESTIONS
 (Deep technical understanding, design decisions, and research-level insights)
================================================================================

--- TOPIC: Advanced Architecture ---

Q50. Explain the role of skip connections in U-Net and why they are critical
     for deforestation detection at fine spatial scales.
A:   Skip connections directly link encoder feature maps to corresponding decoder
     layers. They preserve fine-grained spatial information (e.g., field boundaries,
     small clearings) that would otherwise be lost during downsampling. This is
     critical for detecting small or irregularly shaped deforestation patches at
     pixel level.

Q51. How does the temporal attention encoder differ from a standard LSTM or
     GRU for temporal modeling?
A:   LSTMs/GRUs process sequences sequentially (step-by-step), which is slow and
     can struggle with long-range temporal dependencies. The Temporal Attention
     Encoder processes all time steps in parallel using attention weights, allowing
     the model to directly and efficiently focus on any relevant time step regardless
     of its distance in the sequence, and better handles irregularly spaced acquisitions.

Q52. How does UTAE handle irregularly spaced satellite acquisitions (non-uniform
     temporal gaps between images)?
A:   Each image is paired with its acquisition day (a relative day number).
     These day values are fed into the temporal attention encoder alongside the
     images, giving the model explicit knowledge of when each observation was made,
     allowing it to account for varying time gaps rather than assuming uniform spacing.

Q53. Why is the SAR-specific normalization (fixed VV/VH ranges) preferable
     to learned statistics (ZScore) in some scenarios?
A:   SAR backscatter values have physically meaningful ranges determined by
     sensor properties and land cover. Using fixed physics-based normalization
     is more robust when the training dataset may not be representative of all
     value distributions (e.g., when deploying to a new region), because the
     normalization does not depend on training data statistics.

Q54. Explain potential failure modes of the change detection approach.
A:   - Seasonal variation: SAR backscatter changes with soil moisture, vegetation
       phenology, or flooding, which could be misclassified as deforestation.
     - Temporal gaps: If deforestation and regrowth both occur between two images,
       the change may be missed entirely.
     - Small clearings: If a deforested area is smaller than the model's effective
       spatial resolution, it may be missed.
     - Label noise: Human-annotated deforestation dates may not precisely match
       the actual event, introducing noise into the training signal.

Q55. What is the Dice Loss and why might it be used alongside CrossEntropy for
     this task?
A:   Dice Loss = 1 - 2*(|Pred INTERSECT GT|)/(|Pred| + |GT|).
     It directly optimizes the F1/overlap score and is robust to class imbalance.
     Since deforested pixels are rare compared to forest pixels, Dice Loss encourages
     the model to correctly predict the minority class. CrossEntropy alone may lead
     the model to ignore rare classes.

Q56. What is Focal Loss and what problem does it address?
A:   Focal Loss = -alpha*(1-p)^gamma * log(p).
     It down-weights the contribution of easy, well-classified examples and focuses
     training on hard, misclassified examples. For deforestation detection (class
     imbalance), it helps the model focus on confusing boundary pixels rather than
     being dominated by the large number of easy forest pixels.

Q57. How would you mathematically justify the choice of +/-30 error days in
     _segment_wise?
A:   The +/-30 days buffer approximates the typical revisit time of Sentinel-1
     (6-12 days per orbit) multiplied by an uncertainty factor. Deforestation
     labels from reference datasets (e.g., PRODES) often have monthly resolution,
     so a +/-30-day window ensures images that temporally overlap with the event
     are included. It is a design hyperparameter that trades off between including
     relevant context and reducing noisy, irrelevant observations.

Q58. What are the limitations of using BraDD-S1TS as a benchmark for future work?
A:   - Geographic bias: All samples are from the Brazilian Amazon, limiting
       generalizability to other tropical forests (Congo, Southeast Asia).
     - Sensor-specific: Only Sentinel-1 C-Band; different results possible with
       L-Band SAR (ALOS-2) or multi-spectral sensors.
     - Label source dependency: Labels from existing deforestation databases
       may contain errors or temporal imprecision.
     - Cloud-free optical data exclusion: Fusing SAR with optical imagery
       (when available) could improve performance further.

Q59. If you had to extend this project to detect not just deforestation but
     also forest degradation (partial canopy loss), what changes would you make?
A:   1. Reframe as a multi-class segmentation problem (Forest / Degraded / Deforested).
     2. Acquire degradation-labeled data (harder to annotate than clear-cuts).
     3. Potentially add Sentinel-2 optical imagery in cloud-free windows as a
        complementary modality.
     4. Incorporate canopy density metrics (e.g., L-Band SAR backscatter or
        LiDAR-derived CHM) since C-Band SAR is less sensitive to partial canopy loss.
     5. Adjust the loss function to handle ordinal class relationships
        (Forest > Degraded > Deforested).

Q60. How does the model generalize across different deforestation patterns
     (e.g., large agricultural clearing vs. selective logging)?
A:   UTAE learns spatial features at multiple scales through its encoder-decoder
     structure and attends to temporally relevant observations. Because BraDD-S1TS
     includes approximately 25,000 samples covering varied deforestation patterns
     across the Brazilian Amazon, the model is exposed to diverse signatures during
     training. However, generalization is not guaranteed outside the training
     distribution — this is why the dataset includes both "close" and "far" splits
     to test geographic generalization.

Q61. What would happen if you trained this model on a dataset where the class
     imbalance is extreme (e.g., 99% forest, 1% deforestation)?
A:   The model would likely achieve high accuracy by predicting everything as
     "forest," but completely fail at detecting deforestation (IoU ~= 0 for the
     deforested class). Mitigation strategies include: class-weighted loss,
     Focal Loss, oversampling of minority class, under-sampling majority class,
     and using IoU/F1 (not accuracy) as the primary metric.

Q62. What is the computational complexity of processing a time series of T
     images through UTAE, and how does _segment_wise reduce it?
A:   Processing all T images together scales with O(T^2) for attention computation.
     _segment_wise breaks the full sequence into smaller sub-sequences (one per
     label interval), each containing only the images within +/-30 days of that
     interval. This significantly reduces the effective T per forward pass,
     lowering memory requirements and computational cost, while also providing
     a more temporally focused prediction.

Q63. What are the key differences between semantic segmentation and change
     detection, and how does this project combine both?
A:   - Semantic segmentation: Classifies each pixel into a category at a single
       time point (e.g., forest or non-forest in one image).
     - Change detection: Identifies pixels that changed class between two or more
       time points.
     This project combines both: UTAE performs semantic segmentation on SAR time
     series, but the ForwardFunction's change detection logic computes transitions
     between consecutive timestep predictions, making it a temporal segmentation
     and change detection hybrid.

Q64. How would you validate that the model is learning genuine deforestation
     signals and not spurious correlations in the training data?
A:   - Verify that attention weights focus on time steps where backscatter changes
       consistent with forest removal (lower VV/VH from smooth bare soil).
     - Perform ablation studies: remove temporal context and check if performance
       drops significantly (proving temporal reasoning matters).
     - Use saliency maps / Grad-CAM to inspect which image regions and time steps
       drive predictions.
     - Validate against independent ground truth: field surveys, high-resolution
       optical imagery checked manually, or PRODES reference data held out
       from training.

Q65. What is the significance of publishing BraDD-S1TS alongside the paper,
     and what are the ethical considerations of making such satellite data public?
A:   Publishing the dataset enables reproducibility, fair benchmarking, and
     community-driven improvements.
     Ethical considerations:
     - Positive: Enables global deforestation monitoring, supports policy enforcement
       (REDD+ programme), benefits indigenous land protection efforts.
     - Negative: Detailed deforestation data could theoretically be misused by
       illegal loggers to identify enforcement gaps or patrol blind spots.
     - The dataset itself is SAR-based and does not include personally identifiable
       information, which reduces direct privacy concerns.
     - Data sovereignty: Indigenous territories are included; consultation with
       relevant communities would be the ethical standard for responsible release.
